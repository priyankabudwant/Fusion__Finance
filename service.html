Becoming a member of a co-operative bank typically involves opening a savings or share account with a minimum deposit, which makes you a part-owner of the bank. The process can usually be completed by visiting a branch or applying online.

Membership provides several benefits including the right to vote in the bank's annual general meetings, the opportunity to run for a position on the board of directors, and a share of the bank's profits. Membership requirements and benefits can vary by bank, so it is advisable to review the specific details provided by your local co-operative bank.

Co-operative banks are regulated by the central banking authority in their respective countries, which ensures that they adhere to strict financial and operational standards. In addition to regulatory oversight, co-operative banks often have their own internal control mechanisms to protect members' deposits. Many countries have deposit insurance schemes that guarantee the safety of deposits up to a certain amount, even in the unlikely event of a bank failure. Co-operative banks also maintain robust risk management systems and undergo regular audits to ensure financial stability and security.

Profits generated by a co-operative bank are either reinvested into the bank to improve services and expand operations, or they are distributed to members as dividends. The distribution of profits is decided democratically by the members during the bank’s annual general meeting..

Members typically receive dividends based on their shareholding or the volume of their transactions with the bank. The primary objective of profit distribution is to benefit the members and support the community, rather than to maximize returns for external shareholders.
